ARK Invest and Unchained published a joint white paper laying out the most rigorous public accounting to date of Bitcoin's exposure to quantum computing. Authored by Dhruv Bansal and Tom Honzik of Unchained and David Puell of ARK, the paper finds that 65.4% of the bitcoin supply is not vulnerable to a quantum breakthrough under current conditions, while 34.6% remains at risk if quantum computers advance far enough to break elliptic curve cryptography. That exposed pool breaks down to roughly 5 million BTC in reused addresses, 1.7 million BTC presumed lost in legacy P2PK outputs, and 200,000 BTC in P2TR addresses. To actually breach Bitcoin's ECC, the authors estimate a machine would need approximately "2,330 logical qubits and tens of millions to billions of quantum gates," a threshold they say "will take a very long time" to reach. The paper structures quantum development into five stages, with meaningful risk to bitcoin holders beginning only at Stage 3 and an existential protocol threat arriving only at Stage 4. The mid-2030s is cited as the consensus window for the first public key break, in line with projections from Google, IBM, and Microsoft. The paper calls for proactive integration of post-quantum cryptography standards, specifically ML-DSA and SLH-DSA, via soft fork, while flagging Bitcoin's decentralized governance as the primary implementation challenge.

PQC Mandates

2028 — NSA CNSA 2.0: National Security Systems must support PQC algorithms for all new firmware and software acquisitions
2030 — NSA CNSA 2.0: PQC required for NSS software & networking equipment — gear where PQC is delivered via firmware/software update (routers, firewalls, VPN)
2033 — NSA CNSA 2.0: NSS cryptographic hardware fully transitioned — devices where crypto lives in silicon: Type 1 encryptors, HSMs, certified ASICs; requires physical replacement + NSA recertification, not a firmware update
2035 — NIST IR 8547: hard deprecation deadline for RSA, DSA, ECDSA, ECDH — the outer bound of the Bitcoin migration window
  • 2027 is the practical "get started now" signal from NIST for federal agencies — planning must be done, active migration underway
  • CNSA 2.0 has two distinct deadlines: 2028 for new acquisitions, 2030 for full software, 2033 for hardware — staggered by upgrade cycle difficulty
  • SWIFT 2030 is significant: if global payment rails are PQC by 2030, financial institutions that haven't migrated face interoperability risk
  • The 2035 NIST hard deprecation of ECDSA/RSA is the single most important date for Bitcoin — after this, classical Bitcoin signatures are formally broken in the standards world
  • Bitcoin's soft-fork activation + migration window needs to close well before 2035 to avoid a forced, chaotic migration

Exposed Public Keys

    The Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP) underpins Bitcoin's public-key security. But it is vulnerable to:
    • Quantum computers (Shor's Algorithm)
    • Potentially classical computers (ongoing cryptanalysis)
    • Seed entropy size doesn't matter against Shor's or classical ECDLP breaks
  • ECDLP: given public key Q = k·G, recovering private key k is computationally infeasible — classically
  • Quantum threat: Shor's Algorithm solves ECDLP in polynomial time on a CRQC; estimates ~10–15 years away, timelines highly uncertain
    • Seed entropy size is irrelevant against Shor's: the algorithm attacks the algebraic structure of the elliptic curve group — given public key Q = k·G, it recovers k in polynomial time regardless of how k was derived or how much entropy the seed had
    • A 128-bit seed and a 512-bit seed both produce a private key on secp256k1; Shor's breaks either with the same polynomial-time complexity — the curve structure is the vulnerability, not the randomness of key generation
    • Contrast with Grover's (brute-force): there, seed entropy does matter — 256-bit seed retains ~128-bit post-quantum security; 128-bit seed drops to ~64-bit (broken). But Grover's is a secondary concern compared to Shor's for ECDSA/Schnorr
    • Implication: no amount of HD wallet re-seeding or entropy increase protects against a CRQC — the signature scheme itself must be replaced (e.g. SLH-DSA, ML-DSA)
  • Classical threat: no known break today, but cryptanalysis is active; unexpected breakthroughs (cf. RSA factoring improvements) cannot be ruled out
    • Seed entropy is equally irrelevant against classical ECDLP attacks: algorithms like Pollard's rho and baby-step giant-step operate on the public key Q directly, solving Q = k·G in O(√n) time (~2^128 operations for secp256k1) — the derivation path or seed size has no bearing on this complexity
    • A classical breakthrough would exploit a structural weakness in the curve or group arithmetic itself; a larger seed would not slow it down any more than it slows Shor's
    • Classical brute-force of the seed (exhaustive search) is the one case where entropy matters — but at 256 bits this is classically infeasible (2^256 operations); this is a different attack vector than cryptanalysis of the ECDLP
  • Key exposure problem: a Bitcoin address only reveals its public key at spend time
    • P2PKH (unspent, never broadcast) — protected by SHA-256 / RIPEMD-160 hash layer
    • Reused addresses and P2PK outputs — public key exposed directly in the UTXO set

Attack Vectors

Top address-reuse violators (long-range attack surface):
  • Satoshi's early mining rewards — mined via P2PK; public key is in the scriptPubKey of the UTXO, permanently visible without ever needing to spend. Estimated ~1M BTC. Never moved.
  • Mt. Gox cold wallets — well-catalogued on-chain; large P2PKH balances with previously broadcast transactions exposing public keys. ~140K BTC in trustee-held addresses.
  • Exchanges (older infrastructure) — Binance, Coinbase, Kraken, and others have historically consolidated to well-known hot wallet addresses that have sent and received thousands of times, exposing public keys repeatedly.
  • Donation / static addresses — arguably the most widespread category; any website, GitHub profile, book, or YouTube description listing a single BTC address indefinitely. Every spend from that address exposes the public key.
  • Silk Road / seized government wallets — US DOJ, IRS-CI, and other agencies hold seized Bitcoin at known addresses with exposed public keys.
  • Early wallet users (pre-BIP32) — wallets before HD derivation (pre-2013) didn't rotate addresses; many long-term holders have significant balances at reused addresses from this era.
  • Mining pool payout addresses — miners who used the same payout address for years have fully exposed public keys with large accumulated balances.
Short-range note: P2WPKH is the primary concern here — the pubkey is hidden behind a hash until spend time, but is exposed in the witness data the moment the transaction hits the mempool.

BIP-360 Status

✅ BIP-360 has been merged into the official Bitcoin BIP repository

Activation Signaling: BIP9 vs BIP8
  • BIP9 — miners signal readiness by setting bits in the block version field during a fixed 2016-block retarget period (~2 weeks)
    • Requires 95% of blocks to signal within a single period to reach LOCKED_IN
    • If threshold is not met before the timeout height, the deployment fails — process must restart
    • Gives miners effective veto power; controversial after SegWit activation standoff (2016–2017)
  • BIP8 — similar version-bit signaling, but adds a lockinontimeout (LOT) parameter
    • LOT=false: behaves like BIP9 — times out if threshold not met
    • LOT=true: forces LOCKED_IN at the end of the timeout period regardless of miner signaling
    • LOT=true is contentious — effectively removes miner veto but risks chain split if miners resist
  • Speedy Trial (used for Taproot, 2021) — a short BIP9 window (~3 months) with a lower 90% threshold; gave miners a fast on-ramp with a clear expiry
  • State machine: DEFINED → STARTED → LOCKED_IN → ACTIVE (one grace period after LOCKED_IN for node operators to upgrade)
  • For BIP-360: activation mechanism has not yet been chosen; choice of BIP8/BIP9/Speedy Trial will itself be a major community debate given the political difficulty of consensus changes post-Taproot
  • Economic nodes matter too: even after ACTIVE, adoption requires exchanges, wallets, and major services to enforce the new rules — miner activation alone does not guarantee economic consensus

P2MR Future Add-On: SLH-DSA

SLH-DSA is a hash based PQC scheme; which is ideal because Bitcoin already relies on the efficacy of hash functions. BIP-360 Add-Ons leverage OP_SUCCESS127 — a previously undefined opcode that always succeeds in tapscript, enabling new post-quantum script semantics (e.g. OP_SUBSTR for key extraction) without a hard fork.

SLH-DSA: Trade-offs

Scheme Public Key Size Signature Size Verification Cycles
Schnorr (secp256k1) 32 bytes 64 bytes ~50K
SLH-DSA-SHAKE-128s 32 bytes 7,856 bytes ~4.7M
These NIST standards are architected for PKI use cases. Not Bitcoin. Much work can and needs to be done to architect and implement a PQC signature scheme optimized for Bitcoin. Block weight implications — a single SLH-DSA input would consume ~31× the witness bytes of a Taproot input, making fees prohibitive at current block sizes. Bitcoin has a 520-byte stack element limit — a 7,856-byte SLH-DSA signature can't even be pushed onto the script stack without a consensus change.

SLH-DSA: Additional Trade-offs

  • No key aggregation (MuSig2-equivalent) exists for SLH-DSA, so multisig costs scale linearly
  • No extended xpubs for watch-only wallets

PQC: Other potential schemes

  • ML-DSA (FIPS 204) : lattice-based — standardizes CRYSTALS-Dilithium
  • FN-DSA (FIPS 206) : lattice-based — standardizes Falcon; ~666 byte signatures
  • SQISign : isogeny-based — SIDH/SIKE predecessor was broken in 2022
  • SHRINCS (Blockstream)
  • .... FancySig 🙂

Evolving PQC Security via P2MR

Imagine a world where both P2MR and SLH-DSA were accepted and activated on the Bitcoin network in 2027 or so. You, as the low time-preference HODLER of the keys that unlock the scripts in tx0, could be assured that your Bitcoin is secured from any CRQC threats from both long and short range attacks. However, PQC is evolving quickly. At any point in the future, you would have the ability to upgrade to FancySig. If FancySig is activated into Bitcoin prior to Q-Day, then you could initiate tx1 by spending via leaf0 of tx0 (Schnorr). If FancySig is activated into Bitcoin after Q-Day, then you could initiate tx1 by spending via leaf1 of tx0 (SLH-DSA)
